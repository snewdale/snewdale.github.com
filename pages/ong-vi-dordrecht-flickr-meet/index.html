How to get there back to top ↑


Route from Rotterdam to Dordrecht Merwekade
by 9292ov.nl

Of course, you can arrive by any means but for a bit of fun, we think taking the waterbus from Rotterdam would be a good idea.

The waterbus starts in Rotterdam at the Willemskade. The Willemskade can be reached via tram line 7 that, assuming Rotterdam Centraal railway station as a starting point, travels in the direction of Willemskade/Nesserdijk.

Be sure to be on time at Willemskade: as the waterbus only departs every half hour, missing it by one minute will cost you half an hour!

Upon arrival in Dordrecht, the restaurant can be seen from the jetty, just a few meters to the left.


The City of Dordrecht back to top ↑

The town that would become the city of Dordrecht was first mentioned in texts from the 11th century and was granted city charter (stadsrechten) by count Willem I in 1220. More importantly to commerce, in 1299 Dordrecht was granted ‘stapelrecht’ (lit. ‘stacking rights’), meaning any cargo coming in by sea ships would need to be unloaded and put on sale in Dordrecht. This resulted in booming business as well as giving an impulse to the arts.

While nowadays Dordrecht is considered an island, this has not always been the case. The wetlands to the south, called de Biesbosch, for example, came into existence during the flooding known as the Sint Elisabethsvloed, when drastic changes to the landscape took place. Dordrecht-as-an-island, then, is a direct result of the Sint Elisabethsvloed of 1421.

Highlight 1: Groothoofdspoort back to top ↑


Groothoofdspoort, Dordrecht
by Stewart Newdale

Groothoofdspoort is, together with the Catarijnepoort, one of the two remaining city gates of Dordrecht. The old parts of the gate date back to the 14th and 15th century, while the tower was added during renovations in 1618. The gates, part of the city’s defenses, became obsolete in the 19th century and all but these two were demolished to make the city more accessible to traffic.

The gate and it’s sculpture of the Maid of Dordrecht (de Maagd van Dordrecht; Sint Sura) mounted above the gate, overlook the water where the river Merwede splits off into the rivers Oude Maas and Noord.

We will pass the other remaining gate, the Catharijnepoort, when we go from Engelenburgerkade onto Hooikade later in the tour.

Highlight 2: Lange IJzerenbrug back to top ↑


Lange IJzerenbrug, Dordrecht
by Stewart Newdale

The Lange IJzerenbrug over the Nieuwe Haven is one of eight bridges designed by then city architect G.N. Itz. The Damiatenbrug we will have crossed earlier in our tour is another example of his work.

Designed in 1855, the bridge measures 60 meters in length, 25 meters of horizontal roadway on both sides and a double leaf bascule spanning the remaining ten meters. Visual inspection on location indicates that the bridge can still be (and has recently been) opened to allow the passage of water traffic.

The structure was renovated in full in 2005, a work that involved basically the whole of the construction: a renovation of the wooden foundations was performed, the roadway replaced and some of the cast-iron pieces were remade to replace damaged ones.

Highlight 3: Grote Kerk or Onze-Lieve-Vrouwekerk back to top ↑


church 'de Grote Kerk', Dordrecht
by Stewart Newdale

The oldest standing part of this church is the Mariakoor (choir), construction of which began in 1285. There may have been older, no longer standing structures that predate those that currently make up the church. A chapel is mentioned in a document from the year 1122, that may have been a forebear to the Grote Kerk. The source didn’t sound particularly certain about this, hence the emphasis.

The tower, standing at a respectable 65 meters in height, has until the year 2000 been the highest building in all of Dordrecht. Construction of the tower started around 1504. Plans for the spire, which would bring it’s height to over 100 meters were abandoned when the tower started to tilt, as the marshy soil was unable to carry all this weight.

Instead of the spire, the four clock faces were added, giving the church it’s current silhouette, a signature screaming ‘Dordrecht’ at anyone only slightly familiar with the city.
